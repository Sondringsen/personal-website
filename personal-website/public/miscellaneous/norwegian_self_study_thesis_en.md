# The Long Arm of Conscience

Both *Crime and Punishment* and *Olav's Dreams* begin with the main character committing a murder. The former was first published in 1866 and was written by Fyodor Dostoevsky. The main character is named Raskolnikov and lives in poverty in St. Petersburg. Olav in *Olav's Dreams* moves to Bergen and can be imagined to be living in the 1500s. Olav's real name is Asle, but he changes his name to cover up the murder and start a new life. The book was published in 2012 and was written by Jon Fosse. Furthermore, both books deal with the consequences of crime, and how human beings cope with such an act. A relevant question is how the portrayal of conscience is handled in the two books.

I read the trilogy for the first time when I was in eighth grade. At the time I did not understand much of the work, and I did not reflect much on the content. From the reviews and the reception of the book, I understood that it was an important and good book, and I thought I would have to read it again someday. The special subject assignment therefore became a golden opportunity to work thoroughly with Jon Fosse's literature. I had heard of *Crime and Punishment* and knew that it stood as a great work in world literature. Dostoevsky is Russian, and I have not read many books originating from outside Europe. I also sensed that it dealt with some of the same themes written about in *Olav's Dreams*. In preparation for the assignment, I read an article claiming that Olav and Raskolnikov are two quite similar people. "He (Olav) lives just as poorly with the killings as Raskolnikov" (Knut Hoem, 2012). After that, I was certain that *Olav's Dreams* and *Crime and Punishment* were a good choice.

Before Raskolnikov even commits the crime, he predicts that such an act is always followed by an illness. His thesis proves correct, and after the deed he is immediately struck by fever and delusions. Furthermore, he swings unpredictably between being indifferent, selfish, and compassionate. At one point he even gives a widow his last coins. When Raskolnikov confesses the crime to Sonya, the widow's daughter, the absurdity of his ever-shifting character is underscored: "You gave away your last penny to others! How could you have killed to rob?" (p. 563) she asks him. Throughout the entire book, Raskolnikov suffers from this illness, and it is only when he confesses to the murder that he recovers. Raskolnikov suffers from deep remorse and guilt, he expresses that he is worth less than a cockroach, and considers taking his own life. The illness can be interpreted as a direct consequence of his actions, and as a way in which his own mind punishes him. Olav, on the other hand, does not suffer from this illness, but that may be related to the reason for the murders. "Everyone enriches themselves in different ways, and I too wanted to become rich quickly." (p. 207). Raskolnikov admits here that he kills for selfish reasons, even though he long tries to convince himself otherwise. Olav, by contrast, kills for the sake of love. He and his girlfriend are expecting a child, and driven by desperation he commits the act. He is aware of the wrongdoing, but wants to build a life for himself and Åsta, and love overshadows his conscience. The reason for the killings means that the portrayal of conscience becomes somewhat different.

Both Olav and Raskolnikov show signs of paranoia. Even though they are affected to greater or lesser degrees by guilt, they both value their freedom highly at first. One day, as Raskolnikov is walking near his apartment, he notices an old man walking in front of him. Immediately Raskolnikov concludes that the man is after him. He tries to confront the man, but to little avail. The next day the man shows up right outside his door, and with a rigid finger points at Raskolnikov and calls him a murderer. Almost exactly the same thing happens in *Olav's Dreams* one day when Olav is on his way into Bergen. An old man is walking behind him, and Olav immediately believes that the man is after him. Olav walks away from the old man and straight into the tavern in Bergen. There he encounters none other than the same old man, who claims he knew of a shortcut and therefore arrived first. "The one who is said to have killed, yes, his name is apparently Asle," (p. 109) the man says to Olav, before continuing a little later: "You are Asle" (p. 112). These two scenes are remarkably similar, and the old men can be seen as physical representations of the conscience that continually reproaches them for their actions, giving them the feeling that everyone knows what they have done. At this point, the portrayal of conscience in the two books is almost identical.

*Crime and Punishment* is well over 700 pages and is very thorough in its depiction of Raskolnikov's reaction to the murders. A stream of consciousness passage can stretch over dozens of pages. The lengthy accounts of what is happening inside Raskolnikov's head, and what he thinks about the murder, make it easier to say something about his guilt and conscience in general. This makes his decision to turn himself in seem logical. *Olav's Dreams*, on the other hand, is roughly 70 pages long, and is described by some as a long short story rather than a short novel (Andreas Wiese, 2014). Very little is revealed about what Olav thinks and feels. Often the only thing we know is how he acts and what he says. When Olav is led to the place of execution, he shows no signs of resistance even though he has a girlfriend and a newborn son to fight for. Without knowing what Olav is thinking, the apathy can feel meaningless to the reader, but can be interpreted as an expression of the same thoughts that tormented Raskolnikov. Thus, the portrayal of conscience is not necessarily the same, but conscience is present as a factor and yields the same result, even though it is described in different ways.

The conscience of both Raskolnikov and Olav means that they several times desire to inflict punishment upon themselves, or for others to inflict it upon them. Raskolnikov even considers taking his own life: "For it would actually be more correct, yes, a thousand times more correct, and moreover more sensible, to throw oneself headfirst into the water and put an end to it all, once and for all!" (p. 435). Olav, too, can seem as though he goes to meet his punishment deliberately, and may appear relieved when he thinks about the punishment awaiting him in Bergen. "It is good weather to walk in, even though he carries a heavy burden, yet the load does not feel heavy" (p. 122). Before Olav leaves his girlfriend, she asks whether he must go, to which he replies: "I must" (p. 161). Åsta knows that Olav feels obligated to face his punishment, and does not stop him from leaving, even though she knows that "now she has seen her man, her love, for the last time" (p. 161). In both books, the punishment received from society is a relief compared to the one a person inflicts upon themselves. Neither of them knows what punishment awaits, but both prefer it over the crushing conscience that is portrayed in similar ways in the two books.

Both books have a religious undertone that becomes even clearer toward the end. When Olav is led up to the place of execution, there are forces within him that want him to resist. "He will make his way back to Åsta and little Sigvald, he will, so no one shall put a rope around his neck," Olav tells himself, but gradually comes to understand that his actions have driven him away from Åsta, and the only way to find his way back to her is to face the punishment. For after he is hanged, he is reunited with his love and child: "Alida takes Asle by the hand and then he rises up and then he stands there holding Alida's hand." (p. 167). Something similar happens with Raskolnikov, who admits his guilt before everyone after Sonya persuades him to do so through her humble example. In the afterword to *Crime and Punishment* it states: "At its deepest level, therefore, 'Crime and Punishment' is a novel about God's grace, rather than a novel about a human being's intellectual journey of development." (p. 760, Brodal, 1993). Both books carry this mysterious religious quality. Whether one chooses to call it piety or conscience makes no difference, because neither can be explained by scientific methods. Both authors make use of the mysterious nature of religion to portray this fundamental quality of human beings.

The two books were written at two entirely different points in time, and concern two completely different people in two different situations. Both commit an act that most of us would not be capable of carrying out, and struggle in the aftermath of it. *Crime and Punishment* and *Olav's Dreams* differ in form and use different devices to portray conscience. Nevertheless, we see that the result of their actions leads to an almost identical outcome. Neither Raskolnikov nor Olav escapes the long arm of conscience. So even though the portrayal of conscience may differ somewhat, the expression is the same. One might say that the motive is different, but the story is the same.

*Crime and Punishment* has endured as one of the greatest novels in world literature, and *Olav's Dreams* received the Nordic Council's Literature Prize in 2015. The citation reads: "This year's winner is a rare and excellent example of how formal innovation can go hand in hand with a content that manages to move across time and place." The books deal with something as fundamental as morality and conscience, and do so with a striking credibility. These are things human beings will never stop pondering, and it is what makes these books relevant today and for a long time to come. Most of us will never commit such a crime, but everyone will do something wrong and feel guilty about it. Both books portray in their own unique way how conscience manifests itself, and there is much one can learn from that. "For narrow is the gate and difficult is the way which leads to life, and there are few who find it." Matt 7:14. Both Raskolnikov and Olav stumble on the path, but ultimately ended up finding the gate to life again. To be human means that one will grope in the darkness; what matters is whether you find the gate in the end.
